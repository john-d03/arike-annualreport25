Annual Report 2025

A note before you begin

This report is a record of a year spent inside other people's homes - a year of bedside visits, night calls, quiet conversations, and the slow, steady work of restoring dignity to people living with serious illness.

Across 2025, Arike Home Care reached 4,214 patients and families across Ernakulam district. We made 12,224 home visits, answered 33,132 calls, drove 69,127 kilometres on electric wheels, and trained 50 social-work interns and 12 international nursing professionals. We did this as a free-care service - open to every household, regardless of means.

The pages that follow are organised around five questions: What did we do? Who did it reach? Who made it possible? What did we learn? And - most importantly - what comes next?

No one should be left alone, deprived of care, simply because they do not have the money to afford it.

About Us

A free-care service, rooted in the home.

Arike Home Care is a community-rooted, professionally aligned palliative care initiative committed to restoring dignity, comfort, and quality of life for individuals experiencing health-related suffering. It delivers comprehensive, home-based care 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, ensuring uninterrupted access to medical, nursing, psychosocial, and supportive services within the familiarity of patients' homes.

Services are provided irrespective of a patient's financial status. By integrating healthcare professionals, social workers, community volunteers and technology-enabled systems, Arike addresses physical, emotional, social and spiritual needs, reducing avoidable hospitalisation and demonstrating that compassionate, community-based care can be both clinically effective and socially sustainable.

About Us05

Vision & Mission

Vision

A world where the dignity of a serious-health sufferer is restored through personalised home-based palliative care for all strata of society, irrespective of socioeconomic status.

Arike offers easily accessible, affordable, and equitable tailor-made solutions for the healthcare needs of the public.

Mission

  • Provide 24/7 professional home-based palliative care, including home visits and teleconsultation, ensuring timely and continuous support.
  • Actively engage and empower communities through neighbourhood network programmes, fostering shared responsibility in healthcare.
  • Reduce the burden of serious health suffering by integrating technology that enhances service efficiency, coordination, and quality of care.
  • Strengthen the palliative care ecosystem through training, mentorship, and capacity-building programmes for healthcare providers, volunteers, and caregivers.
  • Expand access to care by collaborating with government schemes, CSR initiatives, and bio-cluster programmes - ensuring support reaches the most vulnerable.
Care is not a transaction. It is a shared social responsibility - a neighbour's hand, a nurse's call at midnight, a doctor at the bedside.

Why palliative care
matters in Kerala.

Kerala has the highest proportion of senior citizens in India - and the highest out-of-pocket health expenditure. Long lives have produced new, complex needs.

Part II · Context08

Context

A model state, and a new burden.

Kerala is widely recognised as a model state for its social development - high literacy, long life expectancy, and a strong public health system. Those very successes have created new and complex challenges.

Demographic transition

Kerala faces a rapid demographic shift - declining fertility, rising longevity, and outmigration of younger adults. By 2030, it is projected to have 35 persons aged over 60 for every 100 working-age adults. Already, 16.5% of the population are senior citizens, the highest share in India.

Burden of chronic illness

Per the National Sample Survey, 54% of elderly individuals in Kerala live with one or more chronic diseases - rarely curative conditions involving prolonged decline, dependence, persistent symptoms, and psychological distress.

Economic strain

Nearly 55 million Indians have been pushed below the poverty line by healthcare costs. In 2022-23, Kerala had the highest share of households spending over 25% of expenditure on health, and 68% of health spending is out-of-pocket - the highest in India.

Isolation & caregiver distress

Migration-driven family structures have produced widespread isolation among the elderly. Caregivers - often elderly spouses or women - bear the brunt of care, leading to burnout and financial hardship.

The predicament in Ernakulam

  • Palliative services are largely unavailable outside regular working hours (typically 10 am - 4 pm).
  • Patients are often forced to seek expensive hospital care for manageable emergencies.
  • Scarcity of trained palliative care professionals across disciplines.
  • Limited access to specialist palliative care expertise.
  • Inadequate volunteer engagement and weak community linkages.
  • Limited public awareness about palliative care.
  • High cost of care - nearly 60% of providers charge rates unaffordable for most families.
  • Insufficient and inconsistent funding to sustain long-term care.

Although over 85% of deaths in India occur following prolonged illness, less than 2% of the population has access to structured pain relief and palliative care.

The Arike Model

Care without time barriers.

A major gap in conventional palliative care systems is the lack of support beyond regular working hours. Illness and distress are unpredictable, often leading families to unnecessary hospital admissions during emergencies.

Arike addresses this through a 24/7 home-based palliative care system coordinated through a central control room. Patients and caregivers can access medical guidance, nursing support, telemedicine, and emergency interventions at any time - including nights, weekends, and public holidays. This approach enables patients to remain at home, surrounded by familiar environments and loved ones, while still receiving professional care.

Key features

  • Timely symptom management during crises
  • Reduced emergency hospital visits and out-of-pocket expenditure
  • Continuous medical and psychosocial support
  • Emotional reassurance for patients and caregivers
  • Caregiver guidance and training

Equity without compromise

Arike ensures that services remain accessible to all, irrespective of financial background. Families who are financially stable may voluntarily contribute towards service costs. Patients unable to afford care are supported through CSR funding, community donations, and institutional partnerships.

This system promotes dignity, shared responsibility, and sustainability - transforming beneficiaries into stakeholders while ensuring that no patient is denied care due to financial limitations.

Multidisciplinary & technology-enabled

Beyond symptom control.

The care team

  • Doctors trained in palliative medicine
  • Nurses skilled in home-based clinical care
  • Physiotherapists supporting mobility and function
  • Social workers providing psychosocial assessment, counselling, and welfare linkage - integrated psychosocial care supported by MEHAC Foundation
  • Trained volunteers offering emotional and social support

This integrated approach ensures that physical symptoms, psychological distress, social challenges, and spiritual concerns are addressed in a coordinated, compassionate manner.

Compassion, supported by systems

To ensure efficiency, accountability, and continuity, Arike integrates technology into every layer of service delivery. Digital systems support:

  • Real-time patient documentation and clinical decision-making
  • Coordination between doctors, nurses, social workers, and volunteers
  • Teleconsultation and after-hours support
  • Transparent financial and operational management

Technology enables Arike to scale responsibly, reduce response times, and maintain high standards of care - without compromising the human connection that lies at the heart of palliative care.

A scalable, replicable model - designed to be adapted across districts and beyond.

III · Impact

Doctor's home care.

For patients who are elderly, bed-bound, chronically ill, or approaching end of life, repeated hospital visits are physically exhausting, emotionally distressing, and financially burdensome.

Arike's doctor-led home care model was developed to respond to these realities - ensuring that medical decision-making, symptom control, and clinical guidance are available where patients live, rather than being confined to institutional settings.

Doctors also act as clinical coordinators, ensuring continuity between home-based care, hospital services, and community resources when escalation or referral is required.

Key outcomes

  • Timely clinical intervention - assessment and treatment for pain, infections, breathlessness, and other distressing symptoms within the home.
  • Continuity of care - regular follow-up to monitor disease progression and refine care plans.
  • Comfort-centred care - treatment in familiar surroundings, promoting emotional well-being and dignity.
  • Guidance for caregivers - practical instructions and reassurance for complex situations at home.

III · Impact

Psychosocial & social work.

Social workers at Arike function as key facilitators of holistic care - bridging medical services, family systems, community resources, and welfare mechanisms.

Psychosocial assessment & counselling

Each patient undergoes a structured psychosocial assessment to identify emotional distress, caregiver strain, financial vulnerability, social isolation, and mental health needs. Support includes:

  • Supportive counselling for patients coping with diagnosis, decline, and uncertainty
  • Emotional support for caregivers experiencing burnout, anxiety, or grief
  • Crisis intervention during acute emotional distress
  • Referral to specialised mental health services when required

Bereavement support

Support does not end with the death of a patient. Arike recognises bereavement as a critical phase requiring structured emotional care.

  • Follow-up home visits and counselling
  • Emotional support during rituals and mourning periods
  • Referral to community and mental health resources

Welfare linkage & socioeconomic support

Many patients come from economically vulnerable backgrounds, where illness exacerbates poverty and insecurity. Social workers actively facilitate access to government welfare schemes and community support.

  • Assistance with medicines and assistive devices
  • Support for hospital admissions and shelter placement when required
  • Coordination with NGOs and community-based organisations

Food kit distribution

Arike runs a Food Kit Distribution Programme to support families lacking resources - providing essential groceries and nutritional support, sourced, packed, and delivered by volunteers and community partners.

A note on the smallest readers

'Nalla Padam 2.0' - school kits for children of palliative families.

Recognising that illness in the family often disrupts a child's education, Nalla Padam provides essential school kits - bags, notebooks, and basic learning materials - to children from families receiving palliative care services.

By reducing the financial burden on families already facing medical and economic challenges, the initiative helps children continue their education with dignity, and serves as an emotional reminder that their dreams matter, even in difficult circumstances.

Collaborations.

Arike operates at the intersection of medical science and social responsibility. Our partnerships are structured so that no patient is left isolated by their geography or the complexity of their condition.

I · Hospital partnerships - bridging clinical & domiciliary care

District Hospital, Aluva. Arike actively strengthens the public healthcare framework by supporting the District Training Centre in Palliative Care. By providing a dedicated staff nurse, we ensure the delivery of consistent nursing services and contribute to the professional development of the government's palliative care workforce.

Sri Sudheendra Medical Mission Hospital. This partnership focuses on the "continuum of care." Arike's team assesses and manages patients with chronic disease post-discharge to ease their transition home. Sudheendra Hospital serves as a referral point for terminal patients experiencing sudden symptom flare-ups who require temporary inpatient supportive care.

II · Specialised NGO & government centres - holistic support

MEHAC Foundation. Our collaboration with MEHAC focuses on integrated psychosocial care. Arike's social work officers identify and refer patients or caregivers facing severe psychological distress - bringing trained psychologists and psychiatrists directly into the community-based care model.

UDBODH Dementia Daycare Centre. Located in Pachalam, a landmark collaborative effort involving CUSAT, Cochin Corporation, and Geojit Financial Services. Arike provides medical check-ups and personalised care plans; the centre offers cognitive stimulation, music therapy, and physical activities designed to stabilise emotional health and reduce isolation for dementia patients.

MIND - Mobility in Dystrophy. Arike works in tandem with MIND to support individuals with Spinal Muscular Atrophy and Muscular Dystrophy - implementing programmes aimed at social inclusion and dignified community living.

Part VII · Future51

Future plans · 2026 and beyond

Four frontiers.

I · The CRHC ecosystem - scaling the Arike model

The Community Resource Centre in Healthcare (CRHC) will serve as the structural backbone of our expansion across India.

  • 24/7 TeleHealth Control Centre - real-time clinical guidance linking frontline workers to experts for symptom management, emergency triage, and ambulance coordination.
  • Community-based palliative hubs - identifying underserved regions in Ernakulam, then Kerala, and eventually across India, acting as "motherships" for local "spokes."

II · Digital frontiers - AI & the CARE grid

  • State-wide palliative grid - integrating CARE software into a unified state palliative care grid.
  • AI & predictive analytics - moving from reactive to proactive care across registered cases.
  • Care Scribe & mobile integration - patient apps for reminders, resources, and symptom tracking.

III · Knowledge leadership - training & research

  • Arike Global Certifications - partnering with accredited international providers to certify nurses and social workers.
  • Evidence-based advocacy - six years of clinical data feeding national policy advocacy and global protocols.

IV · The Healthcare Incubation Hub

  • Technical structuring - helping Residential Associations (EDRAAC, CREDAI) and smaller NGOs build operational and financial systems.
  • Financial self-sufficiency - guiding organisations toward sustainable revenue models.
  • Global scaling - consultancy for international NGOs replicating the Arike model.

One Health · Neighbourhood Network for Health (NNH)

Arike is supporting the Neighbourhood Network for Health (NNH) - a community-based model rooted in the One Health approach that brings care closer to homes through families, neighbourhoods, professionals, and local institutions. It aims to promote health, prevent disease, reduce unnecessary hospitalisation, and ensure continuity of care - building a sustainable, people-centred ecosystem where no one is left behind.
